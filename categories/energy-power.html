Energy AND Power

The grid goes down and suddenly everything you took for granted stops working. This section covers every off-grid power option worth knowing about: what it is, how it works, and who it actually makes sense for. Read it now, before it gets dark.

Portable power stations are rechargeable battery units that store energy and deliver it through standard outlets, USB ports, and DC outputs. Think of them as giant power banks that can run real appliances. They're silent, produce no exhaust, and work indoors, making them the default choice for apartment dwellers and anyone who wants clean, quiet backup power.

Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion / NMC)
Most Common
The standard chemistry in most consumer power stations. Lithium-ion cells offer a solid balance of energy density, weight, and cost. They charge relatively fast and hold their charge well in storage. The trade-off is a shorter overall lifespan, typically 500 to 800 charge cycles before capacity starts to degrade noticeably. Fine for occasional use and emergency standby, less ideal if you're cycling the unit daily.
500-800 cycle life Lightest per Wh Price: lowest entry
Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4)
Longest Lifespan
LFP batteries last significantly longer than standard lithium-ion, with 2,500 to 3,500+ cycles being typical. They're also more thermally stable, meaning less risk of overheating. The downside is they're heavier for the same capacity and usually more expensive upfront. If you plan to use a power station regularly or want something you can buy once and use for a decade, LFP is the chemistry to look for.
2,500-3,500+ cycle life Durability: exceptional Price: higher upfront
Solar Generators (Station + Panel Kits)
Self-Sustaining
"Solar generator" is a marketing term for a power station bundled with compatible solar panels. The advantage is a fully renewable charging loop: the sun charges the battery, the battery powers your gear. The disadvantage is full dependence on sunlight. Clouds, shade, and nighttime are real constraints. Most useful as a complementary charging method rather than a sole power source.
Renewable charging Best use: extended outages Weather dependent
What to own
  • Power station, 500-1,000Wh Covers a mini fridge, phone charging, and lights for 12-24 hours. LFP chemistry is worth the premium if you expect regular use.
  • Compatible foldable solar panel Paired with your station, creates a self-sustaining loop with no grid dependence. Aim for 100-200W output.
  • Second, smaller unit (200-300Wh) For travel or a go-bag. Your main station likely isn't moving anywhere fast if it weighs 30+ lbs.

Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity that can charge power stations, run appliances directly, or top off batteries. For emergency preparedness, portable foldable panels are the most practical option: they work with your existing power station and can be deployed anywhere outdoors. Fixed rooftop solar is a separate, much larger category that requires professional installation and a different budget conversation entirely.

Monocrystalline Panels
Best Efficiency
Made from a single crystal structure, monocrystalline panels are the most efficient type available for consumer use, typically 20 to 23%. They perform better in partial shade and high temperatures than other panel types. For portable applications where you want maximum power from minimum surface area, monocrystalline is the clear choice. The vast majority of quality portable panels use this technology.
20-23% efficiency Best use: portable, maximum output Price: mid-high
Polycrystalline Panels
Budget Option
Made from melted silicon fragments rather than single crystals, polycrystalline panels are cheaper to produce but slightly less efficient, typically 15 to 18%. They're heavier per watt and perform worse in high heat and partial shade. For fixed installations where space and weight aren't critical, they offer decent value. For portable use, monocrystalline almost always makes more sense.
15-18% efficiency Price: lowest Best use: fixed installations
Flexible / Thin-Film Panels
Ultralight
Thin-film panels use a manufacturing process that allows them to bend and flex. They're extremely lightweight and can conform to curved surfaces like vehicle roofs or backpacks. Efficiency is lower, usually 10 to 15%, and long-term durability is a concern. Best for weight-sensitive applications where you need some solar input but maximum output isn't critical.
10-16% efficiency Best use: curved surfaces, vehicles Flexible form factor
What to own
  • Foldable monocrystalline panel, 100-200W Match it to your power station. Most major brands (EcoFlow, Jackery, Bluetti) publish compatible panel specs.
  • MC4 or DC5525 adapter cables Often not included with the panel. Check your station's input port and order cables before you need them.
  • Flexible thin-film panel (optional) Only if you have a vehicle or van with curved roof space. Otherwise monocrystalline covers all use cases.

Fuel generators convert gasoline, propane, or dual fuel into electricity via a combustion engine. They produce significantly more sustained power than battery stations: enough to run major appliances, sump pumps, or even partial home circuits. The trade-offs are noise, exhaust fumes (outdoor use only), fuel dependency, and regular maintenance. When the power goes out for days, generators are what keep houses running.

Inverter Generators (Gasoline)
Quiet + Clean Power
Inverter generators produce "clean" sine-wave power that's safe for sensitive electronics like laptops and medical devices. They adjust engine speed to match the load, saving fuel and reducing noise significantly. Most run at 48 to 58 dB, roughly the volume of a normal conversation. Lighter and more portable than conventional generators, but typically limited to 1,000 to 3,000W output.
48-58 dB noise level Safe for electronics Price: highest
Conventional Generators (Gasoline)
Raw Power, Low Cost
Conventional generators run at a constant speed regardless of load, making them louder and less fuel-efficient than inverter models. They compensate with sheer output: 3,000W to 10,000W+ is standard. The right tool when you need to run multiple large appliances simultaneously, such as a fridge, freezer, and sump pump. Not suitable for sensitive electronics without a separate surge protector.
3,000-10,000W+ output 65-75 dB noise level Price: lowest per watt
Dual-Fuel Generators (Gasoline + Propane)
Fuel Flexibility
Dual-fuel generators can run on gasoline or propane. Gasoline goes stale in 3 to 6 months and gas stations need power to pump. Propane stores indefinitely and is widely available. Switching fuels is usually a simple dial or valve. Output drops roughly 10 to 15% on propane, but the flexibility more than justifies it for extended outages.
Gasoline + propane Propane stores indefinitely Best use: extended outages
Propane-Only Generators
Clean Burning
Propane-only generators eliminate the headaches of gasoline entirely: no stale fuel, no carburetors to clean, no ethanol damage. Propane burns cleaner, meaning less engine wear and longer intervals between maintenance. Fewer consumer options and slightly higher fuel consumption are the main downsides. If you already have a large propane tank on your property, this is the simplest long-term generator strategy.
No fuel degradation Maintenance: minimal Fewer consumer models
What to own
  • Dual-fuel or inverter generator Size to your actual load. Add up the wattage of appliances you'd run at once. Most households need 3,000 to 5,000W to cover a fridge, lights, and a few outlets.
  • Fuel stabilizer Untreated gasoline goes stale in 3 to 6 months. Stabilizer extends that to 24 months. Add it every time you store fuel.
  • Approved fuel cans (5-10 gallon) Store at least one full tank's worth at all times. Rotate stock every 12-18 months.
  • Heavy-gauge generator extension cords Standard household cords overheat under generator loads and are a fire risk. Use cords rated for the output wattage.
  • Transfer switch or interlock kit Required to safely connect to your home's panel. Prevents dangerous backfeed into the grid. Requires a licensed electrician to install.

Power banks are portable batteries designed to charge phones, tablets, and small electronics via USB. They're the most accessible form of backup power: small enough to carry daily, cheap enough to stock multiples. In an emergency, a charged power bank keeps communication alive. The key specs to look for are capacity (mAh), output wattage (how fast it charges), and whether it supports USB-C Power Delivery.

Pocket / EDC Power Banks
5,000-10,000 mAh
Small enough to carry in a pocket or clip to a keychain. These deliver 1 to 2 full phone charges and are designed for daily carry. Some models have built-in connectors that plug directly into your phone, eliminating loose cables. At $15 to $50, there's no reason not to have one in every bag. This is the power bank you have on you when something goes wrong, not the one you dig out of a closet after the fact.
1-3 phone charges Best use: daily carry Price: $15-50
High-Capacity Power Banks
20,000-30,000 mAh
20,000 to 30,000mAh is enough to charge a phone 5 to 7 times or fast-charge a laptop. Models with 100W+ USB-C PD can charge a MacBook at full speed. Heavier than pocket models, usually 1-1.5 lbs, but still compact enough for a backpack or bedside drawer. The right choice for a go-bag or home emergency kit where you need multiple days of phone power.
5-7 phone charges Best use: go-bags, laptops Price: $50-120
Solar Power Banks
Self-Charging
Power banks with a built-in solar panel. The panel is small, typically generating 5 to 10W at best. A full solar charge can take 20 to 40+ hours of direct sunlight. Think of them as standard power banks that happen to offer a slow solar trickle as a last-resort option. Don't buy one expecting to charge it primarily from the sun.
5-10W solar input Solar: last-resort trickle Best as: bank + solar backup
What to own
  • Pocket bank, 10,000mAh, always carried Your first line of defense. It needs to be in your bag, not on a shelf. Buy one, keep it charged, carry it daily.
  • High-capacity bank with USB-C PD, in go-bag Covers a laptop charge plus several phone charges. Real staying power in a multi-day outage.
  • Multi-cable kit (USB-C, Lightning, Micro-USB) Store them with your banks. A bank with no matching cable is useless.
  • One pocket bank per household member In an emergency everyone needs a charged phone. Sharing one bank between four people is not a backup plan.

Light is one of the first things you notice when the power goes out, and one of the easiest problems to solve. Emergency lighting ranges from battery-powered lanterns that last hundreds of hours to fuel-based options that work with no charging at all. The right answer depends on runtime, portability, and whether you want something rechargeable or something that runs on fuel you can store indefinitely.

Battery / USB Lanterns
Most Practical
LED lanterns powered by AA/D batteries or rechargeable via USB-C are the most practical general-purpose emergency light. Modern LED technology means even small units can run 50 to 200+ hours on low mode. USB-rechargeable models can be topped off from a power bank or power station. Look for multiple brightness modes, a low-battery indicator, and a hanging hook for hands-free use.
50-200+ hrs runtime USB-C rechargeable options Room-filling light
Fuel Lanterns (Oil / Candle)
No Electricity Needed
Oil lanterns, candle lanterns, and similar fuel-based options have worked for centuries and still do. They require no batteries, no charging, no technology. Lamp oil stores for years; candles store indefinitely. Output is modest, enough to light a room but not flood it. They produce heat and open flame, which means ventilation and fire safety matter. A Dietz-style hurricane lantern is the classic choice: windproof, inexpensive, and nearly indestructible.
7-12 hrs per fill Fuel stores for years Ventilation required
Headlamps + Flashlights
Hands-Free / Focused
Headlamps free your hands for tasks: cooking, repairs, navigation, reading. Modern USB-rechargeable headlamps deliver 300 to 800+ lumens with multiple modes, including a red light that preserves night vision without blinding everyone in the room. Flashlights produce focused beams for distance and inspection. A room-filling lantern and a personal headlamp together cover all lighting needs in any emergency.
100-800+ lumens USB-C rechargeable Red mode preserves night vision
What to own
  • LED lantern, 300+ lm, per main room Battery or USB rechargeable, with a hanging hook. This is what lights a room when the grid goes down.
  • Hurricane oil lantern + lamp oil Zero technology required. No batteries, no charging, no grid dependence. Reliable for 150+ years and still works.
  • Headlamp per person, with red-light mode Hands-free light is essential the moment you need to do anything useful in the dark. One per person, always charged.
  • Spare batteries (AA, D-cell) Match to your lanterns. Keep at least two full sets. Rotate stock every two to three years.
  • Glow sticks (approx. 12) Cheap, single-use, no batteries. Useful for marking exits or doorways in total darkness without draining a lantern.

The small items that round out an energy kit. Stormproof matches, EDC flashlights, hand-crank chargers: individually minor, collectively critical. These are the things that fill gaps between your major power sources and ensure you're never caught completely without light, fire, or a way to charge a phone.

Fire Starters
Fundamental
The core principle here is redundancy: have at least two independent methods to start a fire in any conditions. Stormproof matches burn in rain, wind, and brief submersion. Ferrocerium (ferro) rods are metallic sticks that produce extremely hot sparks when struck with a striker blade, typically reaching around 3,000°F. They work wet, cold, and windswept, and a single rod lasts for thousands of strikes. Windproof arc lighters work in conditions that kill regular lighters. At $5 to $15 each, there's no excuse not to have all three types.
Multiple methods recommended Waterproof options available Price: $5-30
EDC Flashlights
Every Day Carry
Pen-sized flashlights designed to clip to a pocket or bag and stay there permanently. Modern EDC lights deliver 200 to 500 lumens in a package the size of a marker. USB-rechargeable models eliminate battery swaps. The whole point is having a light on you at all times: not in a kit, not in a drawer, but in your pocket when the lights go out or you're walking to your car at night.
200-500 lumens USB-C rechargeable Pocket-clip design
Hand-Crank Chargers
Last Resort
Hand-crank generators convert physical effort into electricity. They're slow: 10 minutes of cranking yields roughly 1 to 3% of a phone battery. Not a primary power solution by any measure. Emergency crank radios that combine AM/FM/NOAA weather alerts with a flashlight and USB output are the most practical form. You buy these not because they're powerful, but because they have zero dependencies and work when everything else is dead.
1-3% per 10 min cranking No dependencies whatsoever Best use: last resort only
Battery Supply (AA, AAA, D-cell)
Stock Up
Most emergency gear runs on AA or AAA batteries: lanterns, radios, flashlights, clocks. A stockpile of fresh batteries is one of the simplest and cheapest preparedness moves available. Alkaline batteries carry a 10-year shelf life and cost pennies each in bulk. Rechargeable NiMH batteries are more economical long-term and work in any device that accepts the same size. Modern NiMH holds a charge for 2 to 5 years in storage.
AA, AAA, D-cell cover most gear Alkaline: 10-year shelf life NiMH: 2-5 yrs stored charge
What to own
  • Stormproof matches (2+ boxes) Burns in rain and wind where regular matches fail immediately. Store in a waterproof container.
  • Ferrocerium rod with striker As described in Fire Starters above: sparks at 3,000°F, works in any weather, lasts thousands of strikes. Buy one and it will outlast your kit.
  • Windproof arc lighter (1-2) USB-C rechargeable. The electric arc ignites in conditions that extinguish flame-based lighters.
  • EDC flashlight, pocket-clip 200+ lumens. Not stored in a kit. Always clipped to a pocket or bag, always on you.
  • Hand-crank emergency radio with USB Receives NOAA weather alerts with no power source at all. The USB port gives you a last-resort charge option when everything else is dead.
  • Battery stock (AA, AAA, D-cell) Stock to match your gear. NiMH rechargeables are the better long-term choice; alkaline works fine for rotation stock.
