Energy & Power

Whether it's a blackout that lasts a few hours or an extended grid failure that stretches into days, access to reliable power changes everything. This section breaks down every type of off-grid energy solution — what they are, how they work, and when each one makes sense — so you can make informed decisions before you need to.

Portable power stations are rechargeable battery units that store energy and deliver it through standard outlets, USB ports, and DC outputs. Think of them as giant power banks that can run real appliances. They're silent, produce no exhaust, and work indoors — making them the default choice for apartment dwellers and anyone who wants clean, quiet backup power.

Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion / NMC)
Most Common
The standard chemistry in most consumer power stations. Lithium-ion cells offer a good balance of energy density, weight, and cost. They charge relatively fast and hold their charge well in storage. The trade-off is a shorter overall lifespan — typically 500–800 charge cycles before capacity starts to degrade noticeably. Fine for occasional use and emergency standby, but less ideal if you're cycling the unit daily.
500–800 cycle life Lightest per Wh Price: lowest entry
Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4)
Longest Lifespan
LFP batteries last significantly longer than standard lithium-ion — 2,500 to 3,500+ cycles is typical. They're also more thermally stable, meaning less risk of overheating. The downside is they're heavier for the same capacity and usually more expensive upfront. If you plan to use a power station regularly or want something you can buy once and use for a decade, LFP is the chemistry to look for.
2,500–3,500+ cycle life Durability: exceptional Price: higher upfront
Solar Generators (Station + Panel Kits)
Self-Sustaining
Not a separate battery chemistry — "solar generator" is a marketing term for a power station bundled with compatible solar panels. The advantage is a fully renewable charging loop: the sun charges the battery, the battery powers your gear. The disadvantage is you're entirely dependent on sunlight, which means clouds, shade, and nighttime are real constraints. Most useful as a complementary charging method rather than the sole power source.
Renewable charging Best use: extended outages Weather dependent

Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity you can use to charge power stations, batteries, and devices directly. In a prolonged outage, solar is the only truly renewable power source available to most people. The key variables are wattage (how fast it charges), efficiency (how much sunlight it actually converts), and portability (how easy it is to set up and move).

Monocrystalline Panels
Highest Efficiency
Made from single-crystal silicon wafers, monocrystalline cells are the most efficient portable panels on the market — typically 20–24% conversion rates. They perform better in low-light conditions than other types and deliver more power per square foot. Most premium portable panels use this technology. The trade-off is price: monocrystalline panels cost more per watt than polycrystalline alternatives.
20–24% efficiency Best use: maximum power per area Price: mid-high
Polycrystalline Panels
Budget Option
Made from melted silicon fragments rather than single crystals, polycrystalline panels are cheaper to produce but slightly less efficient — typically 15–18%. They're heavier per watt and perform worse in high heat and partial shade. For fixed installations where space and weight aren't critical, they offer decent value. For portable use, monocrystalline usually makes more sense.
15–18% efficiency Price: lowest Best use: fixed installations
Flexible / Thin-Film Panels
Ultralight
Thin-film panels use a different manufacturing process that allows them to bend and flex. They're extremely lightweight and can conform to curved surfaces like vehicle roofs or backpacks. Efficiency is lower — usually 10–15% — and durability is a concern over time. Best suited for weight-sensitive applications where you need some solar input but maximum output isn't critical.
10–16% efficiency Best use: curved surfaces, vans 360° bendable

Fuel generators convert gasoline, propane, or dual fuel into electricity via a combustion engine. They produce significantly more sustained power than battery stations — enough to run major appliances, sump pumps, or even partial home circuits. The trade-offs are noise, exhaust (outdoor use only), fuel dependency, and maintenance. When the power goes out for days, generators are what keep houses running.

Inverter Generators (Gasoline)
Quiet & Clean Power
Inverter generators produce "clean" sine-wave power that's safe for sensitive electronics like laptops and medical devices. They adjust engine speed to match the electrical load, which saves fuel and reduces noise significantly — most run at 48–58 dB, roughly the volume of a normal conversation. Lighter and more portable than conventional generators, but typically limited to 1,000–3,000W. The best choice when you need reliable power without waking the neighborhood.
48–58 dB noise level Safe for electronics Price: highest
Conventional Generators (Gasoline)
Raw Power, Low Cost
Conventional generators run at a constant speed regardless of load, which makes them louder and less fuel-efficient than inverter models. But they compensate with sheer output — 3,000W to 10,000W+ is standard. They're the right tool when you need to power multiple large appliances simultaneously: fridge, freezer, well pump, space heater. Not suitable for sensitive electronics without a separate surge protector.
3,000–10,000W+ output 65–75 dB noise level Price: lowest per watt
Dual-Fuel Generators (Gasoline + Propane)
Fuel Flexibility
Dual-fuel generators can run on gasoline or propane, which is a genuine advantage in emergencies. Gasoline goes stale in 3–6 months and gas stations need power to pump — propane stores indefinitely and is widely available. Switching fuels is usually a simple dial or valve. You get slightly less output on propane (roughly 10–15% reduction), but the fuel flexibility is worth it. A smart hedge for extended outages.
Gasoline + propane Propane stores indefinitely Best use: extended outages
Propane-Only Generators
Clean Burning
Propane-only generators eliminate the headaches of gasoline entirely — no stale fuel, no carburetors to clean, no ethanol damage. Propane burns cleaner, which means less engine wear and longer intervals between maintenance. The downside is fewer consumer options and slightly higher fuel consumption rates. If you already have a large propane tank on your property, this is the simplest long-term generator strategy.
No fuel degradation Maintenance: minimal Fewer consumer models

Power banks are portable batteries designed to charge phones, tablets, and small electronics via USB. They're the most accessible form of backup power — small enough to carry daily, cheap enough to stock multiples. In an emergency, a charged power bank means communication stays alive. The key specs are capacity (mAh), output wattage (how fast it charges), and whether it has USB-C Power Delivery.

Pocket / EDC Power Banks
5,000–10,000 mAh
Small enough to carry in a pocket or clip to a keychain. These deliver 1–2 full phone charges and are designed for daily carry — the power bank you have on you when something goes wrong, not the one you dig out of a closet. Some models have built-in connectors that plug directly into your phone, eliminating the need for cables entirely. At $15–50, there's no reason not to have one in every bag.
1–3 phone charges Best use: daily carry Price: $15–50
High-Capacity Power Banks
20,000–30,000 mAh
These are the workhorses. 20,000–30,000mAh is enough to charge a phone 5–7 times or fast-charge a laptop. Models with 100W+ USB-C PD can charge a MacBook at full speed. Heavier than pocket models — usually 1–1.5 lbs — but still compact enough for a backpack or bedside drawer. The right choice for a go-bag or home emergency kit where you need multiple days of phone power.
5–7 phone charges Best use: go-bags, laptops Price: $50–120
Solar Power Banks
Self-Charging
Power banks with built-in solar panels. Sounds great in theory — and they do work — but the built-in panels are tiny, typically generating 5–10W at best. A full solar charge can take 20–40+ hours of direct sunlight. They're best used as standard power banks that happen to have a slow solar trickle as a last-resort backup. Don't buy one expecting to charge it primarily from the sun — buy it as a power bank that can also absorb a bit of solar over time.
5–10W solar input Solar: last-resort trickle Best as regular bank + backup

Light is one of the first things you notice when the power goes out — and one of the easiest problems to solve. Emergency lighting ranges from battery-powered lanterns that last hundreds of hours to fuel-based options that work with no charging at all. The right choice depends on runtime needs, portability, and whether you want something rechargeable or something that runs on fuel you can store indefinitely.

Battery / USB Lanterns
Most Practical
The modern default. Battery-powered LED lanterns are bright, safe, and efficient. Rechargeable models charge via USB and can double as phone chargers. Disposable-battery models (AA, D-cell) offer long shelf life and instant readiness. LED technology means a set of D batteries can power a lantern for 50–100+ hours on low settings. Safe for indoor use, no fire risk, kid-friendly.
50–100+ hrs runtime 100–1,000 lumens Indoor safe
Solar Lanterns
Renewable
Solar lanterns charge during the day and provide light at night — a self-sustaining cycle that doesn't depend on batteries or fuel. Some models are inflatable and pack completely flat, making them ideal for go-bags. Output tends to be lower than battery lanterns (50–200 lumens typical), and you're dependent on daytime sun exposure. Best as a complement to battery lanterns, not a replacement.
50–200 lumens typical Solar rechargeable Some pack flat
Fuel Lanterns (Oil / Candle)
No Electricity Needed
Oil lanterns, candle lanterns, and similar fuel-based options have worked for centuries and still do. They require no batteries, no charging, no technology. Lamp oil stores for years, candles store indefinitely. Output is modest — enough to light a room, not flood it — and they produce heat and open flame, which means ventilation and fire safety matter. A Dietz-style hurricane lantern is the classic choice: windproof, cheap, and nearly indestructible.
7–12 hrs per fill Fuel stores for years Ventilation required
Headlamps & Flashlights
Hands-Free / Focused
Headlamps free your hands for tasks — cooking, repairs, navigation, reading. Modern USB-rechargeable headlamps deliver 300–800+ lumens with multiple modes including red light for preserving night vision. Flashlights serve a different role: focused beams for distance and inspection. In an emergency, having both a room-filling lantern and a personal headlamp covers all your lighting needs.
100–800+ lumens USB-C rechargeable Red mode for night vision

The small items that round out an energy kit. Stormproof matches, EDC flashlights, hand-crank chargers — individually minor, collectively critical. These are the things that fill gaps between your major power sources and ensure you're never caught completely without light, fire, or a way to charge a phone.

Fire Starters
Fundamental
Stormproof matches, ferrocerium rods, and windproof lighters. These are redundancy items — you should have at least two different fire-starting methods in any kit. Stormproof matches burn in rain, wind, and brief submersion. Ferro rods produce sparks at 3,000°F and last for thousands of strikes. Windproof lighters work in conditions that kill regular Bics. At $5–15 each, stockpile them.
Multiple methods recommended Waterproof options Price: $5–30
EDC Flashlights
Every Day Carry
Pen-sized flashlights designed to clip to a pocket or bag and stay there permanently. Modern EDC lights deliver 200–500 lumens in a package the size of a marker. USB-rechargeable models eliminate battery swaps. The whole point is having a light on you at all times — not in a kit, not in a drawer, but in your pocket when the lights go out or you're walking to your car at night.
200–500 lumens Best use: always on you USB-C rechargeable
Hand-Crank & Dynamo Devices
Zero Dependency
Hand-crank radios, flashlights, and phone chargers convert physical effort into electricity. They're slow and tiring to use — a minute of cranking might give you a few minutes of phone time — but they have zero dependencies. No batteries, no fuel, no sun. They work when everything else is dead. Best kept as a last-resort option alongside better primary sources. Emergency crank radios that combine AM/FM/NOAA with a flashlight and USB output are the most practical form.
No dependencies Output: slow Best use: last resort
Battery Supply (AA & AAA)
Stockpile Essential
Most emergency gear runs on AA or AAA batteries — lanterns, radios, flashlights, remote controls, clocks. Having a deep stockpile of fresh alkaline batteries is one of the simplest and cheapest preparedness moves you can make. Alkaline batteries have a 10-year shelf life and cost pennies per unit in bulk. Buy them in quantity, store them cool and dry, and rotate stock every few years. They're not exciting, but when everything else is dead, batteries are currency.
10-year shelf life Best use: stockpile in bulk Price: pennies per cell
